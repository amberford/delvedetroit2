Timeline Overview

  • 1890
    Hazen S. Pingree, a shoe manufacturer, is elected mayor of Detroit
  • 1893
    Detroiter Marie Owen becomes the first policewoman in the United States
  • 1893
    The 15-ton, 25-foot tall Garland stove was built by the Michigan Stove Company in 1893. It was the largest stove in the world.
  • 1894
    Mayor Pingree establishes vegetable gardens for the poor, which comes to be known as Pingree's Potato Patches
  • 1895
    Detroit’s municipal power station begins to supply power for streetlights and public buildings
  • 1896
    The last horse-drawn street cars are replaced by electric trolleys
  • 1896
    The Detroit Public Schools establish Kindergartens.
  • 1896
    On June 4th Henry Ford test drove his first car, using engine parts borrowed from Charles Brady King.
  • 1898
    Henry Ford organizes the Detroit Automobile Co. with backing from local investors. It fails three years later after only producing 2 cars

    Detroit’s population is 285,704. It is the 13th largest city in the United States
  • 1900
    Nearly 12% of Detroit’s population doesn’t speak English, the highest percentage in the nation
  • 1900
    The Detroit Tigers become members of the new American Baseball League
  • 1901
    Henry Ford establishes his second automobile company, the Henry Ford Co.
  • 1901
    Ransom E. Olds produces the first practical American car. It can reach speeds of 18 miles per hour and costs $650.

Pingree Years

Hazen S. Pingree served as Detroit's mayor from 1890 to 1897, leaving office only after being elected governor of Michigan. He was named the fourth best mayor in all of American history in a poll of scholars for Melvin Holli’s 1999 book, “The American Mayor.”

His tough-love form of social reform, historians note, was the forerunner for the reforms of the Progressive Era. The Republican mayor is best known for turning vacant land into vegetable patches to feed the city’s needy during the economic downturn of the 1890s.

Pingree promoted the regulation of railroad rates, equal taxation, and municipal ownership of public utilities. He also supported the direct election of U.S. senators, an eight-hour workday, an income tax, primary elections and the abolition of child labor. There is a statue of him in the Grand Circus Park in Detroit, commemorating him as "The Idol of the People." He died in London, England in 1901.
