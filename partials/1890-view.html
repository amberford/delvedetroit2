Timeline Overview

  • 1890
    Hazen S. Pingree, a shoe manufacturer, is elected mayor of Detroit
  • 1892
    Four Detroit streets (Jefferson, Lafayette, Cass and Second) - are paved with asphalt, other streets are only paved with cobblestone, cedar block or unpaved
  • 1893
    Detroiter Mrs. Marie Owen becomes the first policewoman in the United States
  • 1894
    Mayor Pingree establishes vegetable gardens for the poor, which comes to be known as Pingree's Potato Patches
  • 1895
    Detroit’s municipal power station begins to supply power for streetlights and public buildings
  • 1896
    Charles Brady King drives Detroit’s first gasoline powered car down Woodward Avenue
  • 1896
    The last horse-drawn street cars are replaced by electric trolleys
  • 1896
    The new Central High School building at Cass and Warren opens
  • 1897
    Sebastian S. Kresge opens his 5 and 10 cent store with J.G. McCrory, it develops into present day Kmart
  • 1897
    Mayor Pingree is elected governor of Michigan, forcing him to resign as Detroit's mayor
  • 1898
    Henry Ford organizes the Detroit Automobile Co. with backing from local investors. It fails three years later after only producing 2 cars
  • 1899
    Ransom E. Olds opens Detroit's first automobile manufacturing plant
  • 1900
    Detroit’s population is 285,704. It is the 13th largest city in the United States
  • 1900
    Nearly 12% of Detroit’s population doesn’t speak English, the highest percentage in the nation
  • 1900
    The Detroit Tigers become members of the new American Baseball League
  • 1900
    Detroit is the world’s largest manufacturer of heating and cooking stoves
  • 1901
    Henry Ford establishes his second automobile company, Henry Ford Co.

Pingree Years

Hazen S. Pingree served as Detroit mayor from 1890 to 1897, leaving office only after being elected governor of Michigan. Old Ping, as he was known, was named one of the 10 best mayors in U.S. history in a poll of scholars for Melvin Holli’s 1999 book, “The American Mayor.”

In it, Holli writes that Pingree’s “role as an advanced social reformer was unmatched by any big-city mayor in the last half of the 19th century.” His tough-love form of social reform, historians note, was the forerunner for the reforms of the Progressive Era. Besides his social reforms, the Republican mayor is best known for turning vacant land into vegetable patches to feed the city’s needy during the economic downturn of the 1890s.
