Detroit's Golden Age

This was a time of great economic growth in America; and it was especially good to Detroit. The city underwent its most rapid growth in the 1920s--not coincidentally, the same period when automobile production skyrocketed. Detroit continued to be a magnet for newcomers until the early 1950s, when the city's population peaked at nearly two million.

David Whitney Building

The building is named in honor of David Whitney Jr.responsible for much of Detroits early commercial and industrial development.He was one of Detroit’s wealthiest men and largest landowners. What made the building stand out was its dramatic, skylight-covered, four-story atrium lobby this building is one of the first of its era and symbolic of the beggining of Detroit's heyday.

The Purple Gang

The Purple Gang was Detroit’s most notorious organized crime gang in the 1920s and 1930s. The Purple Gang was made up of immigrants from Detroit’s lower east side. The “Purple Gang” intimidated and blackmailed witnesses of crimes and victims families. Criminal justice entities were rendered useless as they tried to control this gang's influence on Detroit.

Prohibition

The Purple Gang began by hijacking alcohol smuggled by others across the Canadian border during prohibition. Al Capone, the notorious Chicago gangster, chose to use the Purple Gang to supply whisky rather than battle them for Detroit territory.

Streetcars

In the 1920s Detroit has the largest streetcar system in the nation. The streetcar system was originally run by independent private companies and then were consolidated under the Detroit United Railway company. The City of Detroit started its own public railway company in the 1920s and was a pioneer in public transit.

Eastern Market

Eastern Market grew greatly in the 1920s and additional sheds were added to facilitate that growth. The market presently covers 43 acres and is the largest historic public market district in the United States. Eastern Market is a selling point for a wide variety of produce, meat, spice and other products. It is particularly busy on Saturdays, when farmers tend to bring in their poultry and livestock, along with fresh produce, for sale.
